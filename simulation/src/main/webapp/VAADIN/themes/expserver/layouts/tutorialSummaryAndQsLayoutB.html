Simulation Instructions

Where we will learn how to play the game

Summary

Thank you again for taking the time to play the experiment game. We'll give a short summary of the instructions.

Your Job: The F1 Team Project

You are the Second Engineer on the Formula 1 car project. This means that you will work on the car after your teammate (the First Engineer). Each part of the car your teammate works on affects your ability to reach the goal and get 1st place in the next race.

For example, if your teammate works extra on the Engine, it will be easier for you to reach the team goal for Top Speed. If your teammate does only the minimum amount of work on the Engine, you will have to work longer to reach the team goal for Top Speed. You might not even be able to reach the goal.

If you are not able to reach the team goals you will do poorly in the races. Your teammate could have helped by working longer, but they chose to spend their time on something else.

The $20 competition

In order to simulate the importance your managers place on winning races, and the bonus they will offer, we will offer $20 to the Second Engineer who has the highest point total after the 8 races. This means that if Alex's team has 75 points at the end, and Brittany's team has 80 points, Brittany will receive $20 as a reward for performance. The experimenters will contact the winners after the experiment is finished and set up times to distribute the prizes.

Note: You are not in competition with your partner (the First Engineer) for the $20 bonus. You are only in competition with other Second Engineers. Your partner is a First Engineer and they have their own bonus, and are only in competition with other First Engineers.

Chat Message System

Don't forget that you may communicate with your teammate with the chat system.

Please note: You may chat about anything with your teammate, but we ask that you not reveal your identity. If you reveal your identity, you will no longer be anonymous, and the experiment assumes that no-one knows exactly who they are playing with. Thank you for your cooperation!

Control questions

At the bottom of the page there are some questions. This is not a quiz, so don't worry. These are called control questions. "Control questions" are a way for the experimenter to demonstrate that the participants were paying attention to the instructions. If the participants don't understand the instructions, their behavior might be based on a faulty understanding of the rules or the situation. This also lets us say to other researchers, "We are pretty sure the participants knew the rules of the game, because they answered the control questions correctly."

Again, this is not a test. You can look back at the summary if you forget some details. And if you are having trouble, please feel free to raise your hand and ask the experimenter for help.
